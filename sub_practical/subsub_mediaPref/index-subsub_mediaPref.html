Tactile Feel

To me, one of the most important things to consider when managing your time is, unironically, the “tactile feel” of the tools you use; the words, in this context, would be used a lot more metaphorically. When I say “tactile feel” I don’t directly refer to how something feels when you interact with it, but rather your physical and emotional reactions to using said thing—good or bad, comfortable or uncomfortable, calm or annoyed, et cetera. It’s important to assess the tactile feel you experience when using the tools meant to help you manage your time as I feel that any sort-of barrier that exists within a tool that prevents you from comfortably using it is not only a barrier to the tool but a barrier to the tool’s purpose(s)—in this case, being your time management!

Both digital and physical media, in my eyes, have different tactile feels. Physical media tends to be more “present” to my senses, especially touch, allowing for more precise control over each one of my actions, like writing notes, drawing calendars, moving sticky notes, et cetera. Digital media, conversely, tends to be more disconnected from my senses since I’m forced to interact with it through an interface, though it allows a lot of things to be done more efficiently and cleanly as these interfaces oftentimes produce outcomes that are predictable and easy to work around.

I, personally, prefer the latter when I’m planning things in advance—like scheduled time—as it feels easier for me to keep my plans organized without the worry of the physical media messing up any of my content. Alternatively, I prefer the former when I’m making short term plans—like reminders on sticky notes—as it allows me to articulate my thoughts easier. Whichever media you’d prefer is entirely up to you—it will likely end up being a matter of deciding between whether you prefer precision or speed.

There are lots of ways to help yourself improve your “tactile feel”, all of which altering how certain senses receive stimuli.

Improving Tactile Feel

One of my favorite ways of aiding my own tactile feel is through using color coding to organize what I’m interacting with. I like to color code different types of notes on my Calendar app to help me get a sense of what an event would involve (i.e. hangout plans would be colored yellow, professional plans grey, et cetera), I like to color code my handwritten notes to assess the different types of thinking I took whilst writing them, and I look forward to, sometime in the future, color coding the containers I store my room’s appliances in to help save time when searching for them. It’s also nice to, where possible, change the relationships of the typography to the rest of the stuff on your screen—different scales, colors, and backdrops can go a long way into making chunks of information more digestible.

Interfaces and Tactile Feel

Different Interfaces could, too, affect how you interact with given media, especially in the digital realm where they can have heavy effects on tactile feel. I Implore you to be very picky about the operating systems and software you frequent—it’s important to find products that both offer the services you would benefit from (like Apple’s easy-to-understand “Airdrop” feature) whilst also having them remain as something that you would actually want to use with justification (like how an iPhone can be enticing because of its haptic features). Tactile feel not only involves your moment-to-moment experience with something, but also how you feel about that thing when you’re done, too!

As you may have gathered, interfaces, though being a small part of the bigger picture of what tactile feel looks like, contribute to the existence of other interactable media, like both physical tools. When I mention “tools”, I’m referring to all the things you interact with to create, like a pen and paper, an ink and a brush, et cetera—everything that is explicitly designed to require user input to create any sort-of output is fair game to me! In using tools that are both financially efficient and tactically comfortable, it might be helpful to consider using a tool’s different versions. For instance, you may find yourself not liking how a pen’s ink doesn’t dry quickly enough when put down on a paper; you should, then, look into different options of pens, like different types, brands, or even inks to try to make your tool more comfortable to use. Improving Your Tactile Feel

I’ve written much about what tactile feel is and what it might look like, but I have yet to talk about how to notice, assess, and improve upon it in the moment. As I’ve alluded to before, tactile feel can be present both physically and mentally, requiring you to be simultaneously weary about both your senses and your feelings.

Again, as alluded to before, physical tactile feel involves your senses—touch, sight, sound, et cetera—and how they interact with the world around you. It’s important to notice when one of your senses is overpowering what you’re trying to do—for instance, if discomfort within your sense of touch is making you hesitate using a tool—as it becomes increasingly more difficult to put your mind into managing your time when you have other external matters bothering your brain. If you’ve noticed great discomfort in some of your physical tactile feel, it’s, then, important to understand your feelings through assessing your tools, environment, and media to help yourself take a step back and collect yourself! Then, you’d work on improving on your physical tactile feel through the many ways you can think of in the moment; among other things, I’d recommend changing the type of tool you use—it’s always good to have a variety of the same tool on you at all times in case you’d want to swap out what you’re using.

On the flipside, mental tactile feel is a little different, more-so involving how your brain may work—I’ll be talking from my own experience, so take these words as pseudo prompts to get you to think about different aspects of yourself. It’s important to take notice to your emotions, especially when they aren’t positive as connecting your emotions to certain tasks, environments, or times in your day is a good first step into helping your mental tactility. When you have time, either after your emotions have passed or whilst you’re experiencing them, it’s a good idea to then step back and assess your feelings, especially if you find yourself getting overwhelmed in any way. Once you’ve gotten a chance to reflect on your emotions, it’s good to take steps to placing yourself in a place that would improve your mental headspace; ask yourself,


Do I need noise?
Do I need food? Water?
Do I need to be elsewhere to where I am already?

All-in-all, improving tactile feel requires a lot of effort, reflection, and even, sometimes, research, but I feel like it takes the barrier you may feel to taking steps to managing your own time. It’s not easy for many of us, but it certainly is possible. Give improving your tractile feel a try—who knows what may happen!
